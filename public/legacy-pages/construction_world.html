Construction Learning

Case Study

Project Snapshot

The learning + talent ecosystem for construction professionals

A unified platform experience for construction upskilling, corporate training, events, and talent discovery — bringing learners, colleges, and companies into one connected ecosystem.

The Problem

Construction learners and employers don’t struggle with motivation — they struggle with fragmentation. Students, professionals, colleges, and corporates often rely on scattered sources for training, credentials, hiring, and networking. Even when learning platforms exist, key steps like discovering the right course, understanding plan value, and tracking progress can feel unclear.

Impact

  • Users struggle to find courses that match their level and niche.
  • Progress tracking feels inconsistent, leading to manual workarounds.
  • Pricing / plan clarity creates hesitation during onboarding.
  • Job discovery and filtering can feel harder than it needs to be.
  • Account + billing settings can be difficult to locate.

Goal

Design a single construction-focused ecosystem that makes it easier to discover learning, choose the right membership plan, track course progress, and connect learning outcomes to careers — across students, professionals, colleges, and corporates.

Solution

ConstructionLearning.online in a Nutshell

ConstructionLearning.online brings construction education and career pathways into one experience — combining e-learning, profiles, careers, and community so users can learn, show outcomes, and move toward opportunities without jumping between tools.

  • Learning + certifications: course discovery and enrollment built for construction skill-building.
  • Progress + outcomes: clearer tracking across course content and completion.
  • Careers layer: job browsing + application flow connected to user profiles.
  • Multi-stakeholder ecosystem: supports students, pros, colleges, and corporates in one platform.

My Role

I owned the UX work end-to-end across research and interface design — translating stakeholder needs into flows, structure, and screens that support learning and career actions.

  • Conducted qualitative interviews with stakeholders to understand workflows and pain points.
  • Created personas and mapped key user flows across students, professionals, corporates, colleges, and SMEs.
  • Designed information architecture and wireframes to validate structure before high-fidelity work.
  • Ran usability testing and documented UX friction (progress tracking, job filtering, billing discoverability).

Design Decisions

Role-based information architecture

Structured separate journeys for students, professionals, corporates, colleges, and instructors to reduce cross-role confusion.

Discoverability of high-value actions

Prioritized course discovery, plan clarity, progress tracking, and job flow visibility to address key research friction points.

Credential and progress transparency

Designed clearer progression and outcomes across learning modules so users can track advancement without manual workarounds.

Mobile-aware interaction model

Kept critical workflows usable on mobile to support on-site and on-the-go learners who rely on quick access.

Target Audience

Students

Seek foundational to advanced courses, internships, and flexible membership plans to launch their construction careers.

Professionals

Looking to upskill through certifications, attend industry workshops, and discover managerial- and specialist-level job opportunities.

Corporates

Need a platform to post jobs, manage candidate pipelines, upskill teams, and network B2B.

Colleges

Gain a dedicated profile for promoting programs, bridge academia to industry via internships and research partnerships.

Instructors / SMEs

Author specialized courses, write technical articles, lead workshops, mentor learners, and develop tailored training programs.

User Research

Qualitative research

I interviewed six stakeholders—students, working professionals, HR leads, and academic partners—to uncover their workflows and pain points around construction‐focused e-learning.

Below are the questions:

  • How do you currently discover and enroll in construction training courses?
  • What challenges have you faced completing online modules or certifications?
  • How do you keep track of your progress and outcomes?
  • What tools or platforms do you use to manage your learning?
  • How do you share or verify your completed credentials with employers?
  • What motivates you to upskill or take new courses?
  • What frustrates you about existing solutions?
  • How do you prefer to receive reminders or notifications?
  • What device(s) do you use most for e-learning?
  • If you could change one thing about your current workflow, what would it be?

Interview insights

  • Many struggle to find courses that match their skill level and industry niche.
  • Progress tracking is inconsistent—most resort to manual note-taking.
  • Users want easy sharing of certificates but existing systems feel “locked down.”
  • Notifications often arrive too late or get buried in email.
  • Mobile access is critical—professionals need on-the-go learning.

Information Architecture

User Testing

We gave five target users a set of realistic tasks in our construction-learning app to uncover pain points, surface UI friction, and validate our information architecture.

Assigned Tasks

  • Sign up and select the “Professional” membership plan.
  • Browse the “Safety Certification” course and enroll.
  • Locate and watch the first module video, then complete its quiz.
  • Use the job-board feature to filter internships by “Site Management.”
  • Open account settings and attempt to update billing information.

Test Objectives

  • Measure how easily users can find and enroll in a course.
  • Validate that video and quiz flows feel intuitive.
  • Ensure job-board filters match user expectations.
  • Confirm users can update personal and billing details without confusion.

Key Findings

  • 4 out of 5 users found “Enroll” buttons clear, but wished for a progress tracker on the course card.
  • Some testers expected the quiz to appear immediately after the video ended—consider auto-nav to quiz.
  • Job-board filters were too granular; users preferred grouping by “Internship,” “Full-time,” etc.
  • Billing settings were buried under “Account”; renaming to “Profile & Billing” improved discoverability.
