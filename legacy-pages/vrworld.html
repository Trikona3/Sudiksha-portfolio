ONE VR WORLD

Case Study

Project Snapshot

Immersive VR environments for skill-building and experiential learning

One VR World is a concept platform exploring how virtual reality can be used for hands-on skill development, enabling users to learn, practice, and interact inside immersive 3D environments rather than passive screens.

The Problem

Neurodivergent individuals—especially those on the autism spectrum—often lack safe, structured environments to practice essential social and workplace skills. Traditional training methods offer limited realism and make it difficult to rehearse emotional regulation, conversational flow, and spatial awareness in a controlled, repeatable way.

Impact

  • Learners struggle to generalize skills from theory to real-world situations.
  • Therapists and caregivers lack consistent, measurable feedback loops.
  • Progress tracking across sessions is fragmented or manual.
  • Engagement drops when training lacks immersion or personalization.

Goal

Create an immersive VR-based learning environment where neurodivergent users can safely practice social and workplace skills, while providing therapists and caregivers with clear visibility into progress and outcomes.

Solution

One VR World in a nutshell

One VR World is a VR-based skill-building application that delivers immersive training modules through a familiar, Netflix-style interface. Users select guided scenarios focused on emotional regulation, conversational practice, and proximity awareness.

  • Immersive VR scenarios for social and workplace skill rehearsal.
  • Real-time feedback to support learning during practice.
  • Customizable avatars to increase comfort and personalization.
  • Progress dashboards to track learning over time.

Support & oversight

An administrator portal enables therapists, caregivers, and content managers to monitor learner progress, review outcomes, and tailor training modules based on individual needs.

  • Session-level progress tracking and reporting.
  • Customizable training paths for different learners.
  • Visibility into engagement and performance trends.

Design Decisions

Scenario-first learning architecture

Structured training as modular, guided scenarios so users can practice one skill context at a time.

Immediate feedback loop design

Prioritized real-time guidance and session feedback to build confidence during social and workplace practice tasks.

Personalization to reduce overwhelm

Included avatar and module-level customization so difficulty and pacing can be adjusted to user needs.

Care-team visibility by design

Added support/admin oversight patterns for monitoring engagement and tracking outcomes across sessions.

Target Audience

Neurodivergent Learners

Individuals on the autism spectrum seeking safe, structured VR scenarios to practice social cues, emotional control, and workplace interactions.

Occupational Therapists

Clinicians who need data-driven tools to assign modules, monitor patient progress, and customize VR interventions.

Caregivers & Educators

Parents, teachers, and support staff looking for engaging training to reinforce social and safety skills outside traditional therapy settings.

Program Administrators

School or clinic administrators who oversee user cohorts, generate reports, and ensure curriculum alignment.

VR Content Creators

Designers and instructors who author new VR modules, write debrief guides, and refine scenarios based on user feedback.

User Research

Qualitative research

We conducted semi-structured interviews with five stakeholders—neurodivergent individuals, occupational therapists, and healthcare providers—to understand their workflows, pain points, and desired features for VR-based social skills training.

Below are the core questions:

  • What challenges do you face in everyday social or workplace interactions?
  • How do you currently practice or support social skills and emotional regulation?
  • Which VR features (e.g., real-time feedback, avatar customization) would you find most helpful?
  • What concerns do you have about using VR for skills training?
  • How would you measure progress and success in this context?

Key insights

  • Users struggle with confidence and need immediate, context-aware feedback.
  • Providers value data dashboards to tailor modules and monitor outcomes.
  • Customization (pace, difficulty, environment) is essential to prevent overwhelm.
  • Gamification and rewards boost engagement, especially for younger learners.
  • Caregivers require simple reporting tools to track long-term progress.

User Testing

Test Setup

We ran moderated usability sessions with our persona Sam Thompson (23, non-binary), using a low-fidelity VR prototype that guides neurodivergent users through social-skills scenarios. Participants were encouraged to think aloud.

Sam’s needs included clear guidance, emotional tracking, relatable scenarios, and visible progress markers.

Tasks & Feedback

Task 1: Log In

  • Login flow is intuitive, but needs a “Forgot password” link and clear error messages.
  • Smooth transition to dashboard after successful login.

Outcome: Users logged in without friction, pending error-handling improvements.

Task 2: Navigate Dashboard

  • Dashboard layout feels minimal and calming, but statistics were hard to locate.
  • Some confusion between “Profile” and “Dashboard” sections.

Outcome: Navigation is straightforward; progress section needs clearer visual hierarchy.

Task 3: Select a Module

  • Grid layout is familiar and easy to scan, though detailed thumbnails and descriptions are missing.
  • Customizable backgrounds motivate some users but distract others.

Outcome: Module selection feels smooth; add descriptions and consider background options.

Key Insights

  • Minor UI tweaks (font size, button placement) significantly affect usability.
  • Grid views outperformed carousels for module discovery.
  • Customizable text size, colors, and help prompts (tutorials) are essential for inclusivity.
  • Voice-command support emerged as a strong secondary interaction mode.
  • Consistent error messaging and clear patterns increase user confidence.
  • Emotional-tracking and gamification features were highly appreciated.

Participant Q&A Highlights

  • Preferred environments: Realistic, calming VR scenes resembling everyday settings like quiet parks, offices, or cafés to support immersion and reduce anxiety.
  • Interaction: Simple point-and-click, gaze-based interactions, and optional voice commands to cater to diverse motor and cognitive needs.
  • Menu design: Large, clearly labeled module cards inspired by Netflix UI for intuitive navigation; tab-based layout reduces cognitive load..
  • Progress tracking: Real-time visual feedback (e.g., completion bars, performance badges) and weekly summaries to keep users motivated and track learning milestones.
  • Customization: Options for avatar setup, UI personalization (e.g., themes, font sizes), and language preferences to accommodate neurodiverse users.
