The Problem

Live debates, lectures, and news events move too fast for traditional fact-checking. Journalists and students are expected to catch every claim, look up numbers in real time, and still listen to what’s being said. Most “fact-checks” arrive hours or days later, after misinformation has already been shared.

Impact

  • Reporters juggle multiple tabs and notes while trying not to miss live quotes.
  • Students struggle to know which statistics or charts are still accurate.
  • False or outdated claims can spread long before corrections are published.
  • Trust in institutions erodes when errors slip through under time pressure.

Goal

Reduce the verification burden by giving people a real-time fact-checking layer while they listen. VerifyLens listens to live speech, highlights factual claims, and surfaces clear verdict cards with sources, so journalists and students can stay focused on the story while still publishing confidently.

Solution

VerifyLens in a nutshell

VerifyLens listens to live speech, pulls out factual claims, checks them against trusted data sources and surfaces simple verdict cards – True, False or Misleading – with citations.

  • <3s checks: near real-time verification while the speaker is still talking.
  • Fact cards: clear claim, verdict, confidence score and sources.
  • History: sessions and claims saved for later reporting or study.
  • Chrome-first: designed to sit beside news streams, videos and lectures.

My role & collaboration

I led the end-to-end product UX: research, information architecture, user flows and the initial Chrome/web app concept, working with AI engineers on a five-week MVP scope.

  • Defined user journeys for journalists and students.
  • Created flows for live sessions, quick checks, history and claim detail.
  • Produced personas, UI explorations and developer-ready IA diagrams.

Design Decisions

Companion model over full dashboard

Positioned VerifyLens as a second screen beside live streams so users can keep listening while checking claims.

Verdict-card first information hierarchy

Prioritized concise True/False/Misleading cards with visible source citations and confidence at a glance.

Workflow aligned to real behavior

Designed around listening → checking → saving flows and included history/export pathways for reporting and study use cases.

Trust-forward visual language

Used restrained color, high legibility, and consistent component states to keep high-pressure verification sessions calm and readable.

Branding & Visual System

VerifyLens is designed to feel precise and trustworthy without being heavy. The brand system uses clean sans-serif type, calm blues, and simple iconography so fact-checking feels approachable during high-pressure moments like live events.

Typography

I paired Inter for web and SF Pro for iOS to keep the product aligned with native platform expectations. Both families are highly legible at small sizes, which supports dense fact cards and live transcription without visual fatigue.

Logo System

The logo combines a shield + checkmark to communicate protection and verified truth. I built a scalable wordmark and icon set that works from tiny status chips to splash screens, while staying consistent in color, grid, and spacing.

Iconography & UI Components

Icons follow a 24px grid with rounded corners so they feel soft but precise. Button styles use a single deep navy primary and a warm accent for secondary actions, making destructive or low-confidence states visually distinct while keeping the core flow calm and focused.

Competitor Analysis

Competitor / Approach Strengths Weaknesses Opportunity for VerifyLens
Traditional fact-check sites
(PolitiFact, Snopes, etc.)
  • High editorial standards and strong brand trust.
  • Deep dives and context for complex claims.
  • Not built for live events or lectures.
  • Coverage limited to a small subset of claims.
  • Surface quick verdicts during events, then link out to long-form articles.
  • Position as a complement, not a replacement.
Manual research workflows
  • Flexible – users can search any site or database.
  • No new tools to learn.
  • Slow and error-prone during live speech.
  • Hard to track which claim came from which source.
  • Automate the repetitive parts: transcription, claim extraction and first-pass checks.
  • Generate structured fact cards that can be audited later.
Mobile AI fact-checking apps
(e.g., Verifi – Fact Checker)
  • Simple mobile UI for consumers.
  • Often include basic verdict labels.
  • Phone-only flows don’t fit newsroom or classroom setups.
  • Limited transparency into sources and confidence.
  • Chrome-first experience designed for laptops and external monitors.
  • Deeper source citations and export tools for professional use.

Technology

Client

React web app + Chrome extension with WebSocket-based live updates.

AI Engine

Whisper for transcription, LLMs for claim extraction and verification orchestration.

Data Sources

Government and academic APIs (e.g., labor, crime, health statistics) plus curated reference datasets.

Backend

Node.js services, caching with Redis, and a pipeline tuned for <3s end-to-end latency.

Challenges & Learnings

Trust beats novelty

Early interviews showed that journalists are wary of AI that “makes things up”. Clear citations, conservative wording and visible confidence scores are non-negotiable.

Designing for side-by-side use

The layout had to work alongside streaming video, slides or Zoom – which drove choices like a narrow panel, collapsible history and non-blocking alerts.

Latency as a UX constraint

The interaction model assumes sub-3 second responses. Anything slower feels like a post-event tool, so the technical architecture and copy both set expectations carefully.

Positioned as a companion

Messaging and UI are framed around “assist” and “support” rather than “replace fact-checkers”, which resonated better with professionals and educators.
