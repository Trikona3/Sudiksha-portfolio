Case Study Summary

This qualitative user research study explored how novice players experience Minecraft without any onboarding or tutorials. Five participants, who had never played Minecraft or open-world sandbox games before, were selected through purposive sampling. Each participant engaged in a 35-minute desktop gameplay session while using the think-aloud protocol to verbalize their experiences. Following each session, we conducted semi-structured interviews to reflect on observed behaviors and capture frustrations, moments of joy, and barriers to progression.

We transcribed all sessions and interviews, then used grounded theory to identify recurring patterns and challenges. Key focus areas included crafting confusion, inventory overload, and poor navigational understanding. Our study's emphasis was to understand player cognition, map friction points, and translate these into actionable design insights for improving early-game onboarding in sandbox games.

Challenges Uncovered

  • Unfamiliarity with controls and no onboarding led to wasted time.
  • Crafting progression was unclear; most didn’t know how to make a crafting table.
  • Water navigation was universally confusing, leading to multiple deaths.
  • Inventory management was overwhelming for new players.
  • Players consistently required external help to progress.

User Quotes

“I wouldn’t have known how to use the crafting table without your help.” — P2
“I just wanted to build my house, man.” — P4

Research Insights

  • Novices showed resilience and adaptation when given minimal guidance, but clearer cues accelerated learning curves.
  • Players benefit from micro-tutorials embedded in real gameplay, rather than static instructions or menus.
  • Observational and verbal data together revealed where UI affordances failed or succeeded in guiding behavior.

Outcome & Design Recommendations

Based on qualitative coding, we mapped behavioral breakdowns into five categories and developed UX design interventions such as onboarding scaffolds, tooltip-based progressive tutorials, and real-time feedback loops. This research offers a grounded framework for evaluating early-game UX in sandbox or open-world games, and highlights the value of in-context, player-led learning.
